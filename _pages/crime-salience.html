Homebuyers observe crime infrequently, recall it imperfectly, and often overweight unusually salient incidents. This paper studies how these behavioral frictions shape the spatial and temporal pattern of crime capitalization in housing prices. Using detailed incident-level crime data linked to repeat-sales transactions in Chicago (2005–2021), I develop a flexible space–time kernel estimator that recovers the distance-decay and time-decay of crime’s price impact. A crime within 0.05 miles of a property in the six months before sale reduces the sale price by approximately 0.20%, but the effect dissipates sharply with both distance and time. The pattern is consistent with salience-driven pricing: buyers overreact to proximate, recent crime and underreact to more distant or dated incidents.
