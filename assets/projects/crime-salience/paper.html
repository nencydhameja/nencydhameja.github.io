Model & Identification

Repeat-sales framework: Prices are differenced within property (parcel PIN), eliminating time-invariant housing characteristics. The dependent variable is Δ ln Pi, the change in log sale price between consecutive sales of the same property.

Crime exposure: For each sale, crime is counted within spatial rings (near vs. far) and temporal windows (1–12 months before sale). The coefficient β measures the price effect of an additional crime in the near ring relative to the far ring.

Fixed effects: Three specifications absorb different levels of unobserved confounds: PIN + CA×Month, PIN + CA×Year + YearMonth, and PIN + Tract×Month. Finer FE absorb more neighborhood-level trends but reduce identifying variation.

Identifying variation: Within-property, within-neighborhood variation in crime exposure across sales. Two sales of the same property in the same neighborhood may face different nearby crime depending on timing and the spatial realization of incidents.

Near vs. far: The near–far difference (δ = βnear − βfar) nets out neighborhood-wide shocks that affect both rings equally, isolating the hyperlocal effect of crime proximity.

Temporal placebo: Crime occurring after the sale date cannot have caused the price. A null post-sale coefficient supports the causal interpretation.

Spatial placebo: Distance-permutation tests reassign near and far rings to check that the effect is specific to proximity, not an artifact of the ring structure.

Estimators: OLS treats all repeat-sales pairs equally. Case-Shiller GLS downweights pairs with long holding periods, whose error variance is larger. Persistence-controlled variants add historical crime counts to separate persistent from transitory capitalization.

Threats: Correlated neighborhood trends (gentrification, disinvestment) that move both crime and prices. Addressed by progressively finer FE and by the persistence control.

Specification

OLS:  ln Pit = βnear · Crimenearit + βfar · Crimefarit + αi + δt + εit
GLS:  Same, weighted by Ω−1
+ Persistence:  adds Pi
+ Heckman:  adds λ̂i

The estimand is δ = βnear − βfar. Since the outcome is ln(price), δ×100 gives the percentage change in sale price per additional nearby crime relative to far crime.

Research Design

  • Treatment heterogeneity: Separate spec curves by crime type and visibility to test whether salience drives capitalization.
  • Outcome heterogeneity: Spec curves by property type to test whether different buyers respond differently.
  • Placebo treatments: Domestic crime and non-spatial crime as falsification tests.
  • Citywide attention shock: DiD using the November 2015 Laquan McDonald video.
Results

Main Specification

Estimator: Case-Shiller GLS with persistence control  |  FE: PIN + CA×Month  |  Window: 5–6 months  |  Near: 0–0.05 mi  |  Far: 0.35–0.40 mi

Headline Effect

Each additional crime within 0.05 miles (80 meters) reduces sale prices by 0.11% relative to crime at 0.35–0.40 miles. Far crime has a zero effect—buyers do not price crime beyond their immediate surroundings.

SF buyers are roughly twice as sensitive to nearby crime as the average property (−0.25% vs −0.11%), consistent with the salience mechanism—single-family homes are street-level properties where crime is directly visible.

Estimator Robustness

The result holds across all 8 estimator variants (OLS, GLS, ± persistence control, ± Heckman selection correction). All variants pass the post-sale placebo test for single-family properties (falsification rate < 5%).

Fixed Effects Progression

  • PIN + CA×Month: Property FE + Community Area×Month absorbs neighborhood-level monthly shocks.
  • PIN + CA×Year + Year-Month: Separates neighborhood annual trends from city-wide monthly shocks.
  • PIN + Tract×Month: Finest geography—absorbs tract-level monthly variation.
